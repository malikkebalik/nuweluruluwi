Amateur Career

Lydia Ko competed in the New Zealand national amateur championships for the first time when he was seven years old. She won the Bing Lee/Samsung Women's NSW Open on the ALPG Tour as a 14-year-old, becoming the youngest player to win a professional golf tour tournament.

She beat Japanese great Ryo Ishikawa's 15-year-old record, as well as Australian Amy Yang's women's record of 16 years, 192 days.

At the CN Canadian Women's Open in 2012, she became the LPGA Tour's youngest ever winner. She became the first amateur to win an LPGA tournament in almost 43 years.

Lydia Ko has 6 wins as an amateur including the Australian Women's Amateur Strokeplay Championship in 2011 and the US Women's Amateur in 2012.

She became the first woman from New Zealand to win the US Amateur.

Professional Career 

Lydia Ko turned professional in 2013 and in her pro debut, she finished tied for 21st at the 2013 CME Group Titleholders.

The LPGA Tour authorized Ko's request to join the Tour in October 2013, disregarding the Tour's rule that members be at least 18 years old.

"It is not often that the LPGA welcomes a rookie who is already a back-to-back LPGA Circuit winner," said tour commissioner Mike Whan in granting Ko's request.

[image-3]

Ko began working with swing coach David Leadbetter in November 2013.

Ko won three tournaments in 2014. On 27 April 2014, she earned her first LPGA Tour win as a professional by winning the Swinging Skirts LPGA Classic.

In July, she won her second tournament of the year, the Marathon Classic. In November 2014, she won her third tournament of the season, the season-ending CME Group Tour Championship.

2015 was a fantastic year for the golfer, as she won five times and rose to the top of the global rankings. On October 26, 2015, Ko became the youngest player to win ten major tour events at the age of 18 years, 6 months, and 2 days.

She break the record of Horton Smith, who set the PGA Tour record of 21 years, 7 months in 1929, and Nancy Lopez, who set the previous LPGA Tour record of 22 years, 2 months, 5 days in 1979.

In 2016, Ko won four times. Her win includes the ISPS Handa New Zealand Women's Open, the Kia Classic, and two more LPGA Tour triumphs at the Walmart NW Arkansas Championship and the Marathon Classic.

2017 was definitely not her greatest year; she competed in 26 events but did not win any. Furthermore, she finished in the top-ten ten times, dropping her year-end global position to ninth.

Ko played in 26 events in 2018, winning once, at the LPGA Mediheal Title, finishing in the top-10 ten times, and without winning a major championship.

In 2019, Ko competed in 24 events, did not win any, and finished in the top ten four times.

In 2020, Ko competed in 13 tournaments, won none, and finished in the top ten four times.
